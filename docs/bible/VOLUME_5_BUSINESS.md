# TradeOS Bible — Volume 5: Business

## Purpose

This volume defines how TradeOS becomes a durable business. It separates verified facts from proposed strategy, founder decisions, deferred choices, and prohibited directions.

**Status labels used throughout**

- **VERIFIED** — supported by current repository or founder evidence.
- **PROPOSED** — recommended strategy, not finalized.
- **FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED** — cannot be treated as policy until explicitly decided.
- **DEFERRED** — intentionally postponed.
- **PROHIBITED** — outside the approved business direction.

## 1. Business mission

**VERIFIED:** Build a practical operating system that helps growing trade contractors move work from customer request to collected revenue with less friction, clearer decisions, and stronger control.

## 2. Company structure

**VERIFIED:** 404 TradeOS is the parent business context; TradeOS is the software product. Agency services and software must remain commercially distinguishable.

## 3. TradeOS product identity

**VERIFIED:** TradeOS is a review-first, AI-assisted contractor operating system, not a generic ERP and not an autonomous financial decision-maker.

## 4. Ideal customer profile

**VERIFIED:** Owner-led or operations-led trade contractors with approximately 5–30 technicians, meaningful estimating and scheduling volume, and dissatisfaction with fragmented or mismatched tools.

## 5. Primary market segment

**VERIFIED:** Growing residential and light-commercial trade contractors that have outgrown entry-level field-service software but cannot justify enterprise cost and implementation burden.

## 6. Secondary market segments

**PROPOSED:** Smaller high-complexity specialty contractors, multi-crew remodelers, and regional service businesses with similar workflows may follow after the core segment is proven.

## 7. Buyer personas

Primary buyers are owners, general managers, and operations leaders. Influencers include dispatchers, estimators, office managers, and accounting staff.

## 8. User versus buyer distinction

The buyer seeks control, margin, visibility, and growth. Daily users seek speed, clarity, fewer duplicate steps, and reliable handoffs.

## 9. Customer pain profile

Common pains include disconnected systems, duplicate entry, weak estimating consistency, unclear job status, slow invoicing, poor customer-document flow, and software that is either too shallow or too complex.

## 10. Jobs-to-be-done

TradeOS must help contractors win work, price accurately, coordinate execution, expose exceptions, create trustworthy documents, invoice faster, collect faster, and understand what action comes next.

## 11. Market category

**PROPOSED:** Position TradeOS as a contractor operating system or field-service and project-operations platform for growing trades businesses.

## 12. Market gap

**VERIFIED:** A gap exists between lightweight products that become limiting as contractors grow and enterprise platforms with high cost, implementation burden, and complexity.

## 13. Positioning statement

For growing trade contractors that need more operational depth without enterprise overhead, TradeOS is a review-first contractor operating system that connects estimating, scheduling, field work, documents, invoicing, and customer workflows in one practical platform.

## 14. Core value proposition

TradeOS should improve revenue flow, margin confidence, operational visibility, speed, and customer trust while reducing owner and dispatcher cognitive load.

## 15. Competitive differentiation

Differentiate through connected workflows, strong estimating and cost intelligence, practical dispatcher tools, transparent packaging, migration support, and AI that prepares actions without taking control away.

## 16. Competitor landscape

The relevant landscape includes ServiceTitan, Jobber, Housecall Pro, Buildertrend, Procore, trade-specific point solutions, spreadsheets, and internal combinations of generic tools.

## 17. ServiceTitan comparison

**VERIFIED STRATEGY:** Compete on simplicity, faster implementation, transparent pricing posture, owner comprehension, and fit for the underserved mid-market. Do not claim enterprise parity before evidence exists.

## 18. Jobber comparison

Compete on deeper estimating, connected project and document workflows, lifecycle rigor, and growth headroom while preserving ease of use.

## 19. Housecall Pro comparison

Compete on operational depth, stronger project and cost workflows, review-first AI, and cleaner cross-module state.

## 20. Buildertrend comparison

Compete by serving both service and project workflows with stronger field-service coordination and a more unified estimating-to-collection path.

## 21. Procore comparison

Do not compete as a broad enterprise construction platform. TradeOS should remain more approachable, trade-focused, and practical for smaller organizations.

## 22. Emerging competitor watchlist

**PROPOSED:** Review AI-native field-service tools, vertical SaaS entrants, accounting-platform extensions, and contractor-specific automation products quarterly.

## 23. Competitive response policy

Do not copy features reactively. Validate whether a competitor capability advances a core contractor outcome before adding it to the roadmap.

## 24. Packaging philosophy

Packaging must be understandable, aligned to customer maturity, and avoid forcing small contractors into enterprise-style negotiations.

## 25. Pricing principles

Pricing should reflect clear value, remain transparent, support sustainable service, and avoid surprise implementation or mandatory add-on charges.

## 26. Current proposed pricing

**FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED:** Product pricing is not finalized in current repository evidence. Any figures discussed previously must remain labeled proposed until approved in a pricing decision record.

## 27. Pricing assumptions

**PROPOSED:** Price may vary by organization size, active users or technicians, enabled modules, AI usage, support level, and migration complexity.

## 28. Packaging tiers

**PROPOSED:** Use a simple good/better/best structure or a core platform with clear module expansion. Final names, limits, and prices require founder approval.

## 29. Add-ons and usage-based possibilities

Potential add-ons include advanced migration, premium onboarding, high-volume messaging, payment processing, premium support, and high-cost AI usage. Avoid excessive nickel-and-diming.

## 30. Free trial or demo strategy

**FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED:** Prefer a guided demo and assisted pilot during early launch. Self-service trials should wait until onboarding and sample-data flows are reliable.

## 31. Sales motion

**PROPOSED:** Begin founder-led, relationship-driven, and workflow-led. Scale sales only after repeatable qualification, demo, onboarding, and retention evidence.

## 32. Founder-led sales process

Use direct contractor conversations, diagnose workflow pain, demonstrate the relevant path, agree on a pilot outcome, and document objections and lost-deal reasons.

## 33. Demo structure

Show the owner morning view, customer/project context, estimate-to-proposal path, scheduling/dispatch, invoice readiness, and review-first AI. Do not fake unsupported capabilities.

## 34. Qualification criteria

Qualify for pain severity, team size, current tools, implementation willingness, data quality, decision authority, urgency, and alignment with supported workflows.

## 35. Discovery-call framework

Ask how work enters the business, where estimates are built, how jobs are assigned, how field evidence returns, how invoices are triggered, where delays occur, and what software currently fails.

## 36. Objection handling

Respond with evidence, limitations, migration clarity, and pilot outcomes. Do not promise roadmap features as completed functionality.

## 37. Proposal and closing process

Summarize agreed problems, implementation scope, supported workflows, responsibilities, pricing, exclusions, success criteria, and pilot or subscription terms.

## 38. Customer onboarding

Onboarding must establish organization settings, users, roles, customers, cost data, branding, workflow defaults, and a first useful end-to-end outcome.

## 39. Data migration expectations

Migration scope must be explicit. Start with customers, contacts, active projects/jobs, costbook data, and open financial documents where supported. Preserve source exports and reconciliation evidence.

## 40. Implementation and training

Use role-based training with real contractor workflows. Avoid generic feature tours that do not produce operational competence.

## 41. Support model

**PROPOSED:** Early customers receive high-touch support. Support must track severity, response, resolution, product defect linkage, and repeated training gaps.

## 42. Customer success

Success means repeated completion of valuable workflows, trusted data, expanding team adoption, and measurable reduction in operational friction.

## 43. Retention strategy

Drive retention through reliable daily workflows, useful owner visibility, fast support, data portability, and continuing evidence that TradeOS improves business operations.

## 44. Expansion and upsell

Expansion should follow real usage: more technicians, modules, locations, messaging, automation, migration, or premium support—not artificial restrictions.

## 45. Churn signals

Watch falling weekly usage, incomplete onboarding, unsupported workflows, recurring defects, declining document volume, manual workarounds, and unresolved executive concerns.

## 46. Beta program

**VERIFIED:** Known contractors willing to give direct feedback are the preferred initial beta group.

## 47. Pilot-customer selection

Choose customers whose workflows match current product scope, who can provide honest feedback, and who will complete real work rather than only explore screens.

## 48. Beta incentives

**FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED:** Options include discounted early access, founder support, migration assistance, influence on priorities, or protected introductory pricing.

## 49. Beta feedback loop

Capture observations, defects, workarounds, desired outcomes, severity, frequency, user role, and whether feedback reflects a core need or one-off preference.

## 50. Launch strategy

Launch in controlled stages: internal readiness, founder preview, design partners, closed beta, paid pilot, limited release, then broader availability.

## 51. Geographic rollout

**PROPOSED:** Begin regionally where founder relationships and support are strongest, then expand based on onboarding and support capacity.

## 52. Trade-by-trade rollout

Prioritize trades whose estimating, scheduling, and document workflows fit the current domain model. Do not claim universal trade coverage prematurely.

## 53. Marketing strategy

Lead with contractor outcomes, real workflow proof, migration confidence, transparent limitations, and founder credibility.

## 54. Brand message

TradeOS should sound practical, confident, contractor-aware, and direct—not like generic enterprise software or an AI gimmick.

## 55. Website message hierarchy

Lead with target customer and outcome, then workflow proof, differentiation, product modules, trust, migration, pricing posture, and a clear demo action.

## 56. Content strategy

Create useful material around estimating accuracy, dispatch friction, owner visibility, invoicing speed, migration, AI trust, and contractor workflow improvement.

## 57. Local and regional acquisition

Use founder relationships, contractor referrals, local search, regional associations, trade events, and direct outreach before broad paid acquisition.

## 58. Partnerships

Potential partners include accounting advisors, contractor consultants, distributors, trade associations, web agencies, migration specialists, and payment providers.

## 59. Associations and trade groups

**PROPOSED:** Join or partner selectively where access to the target segment and product feedback justify the cost.

## 60. Referral strategy

Reward credible introductions and customer advocacy without encouraging misleading endorsements.

## 61. Channel strategy

**DEFERRED:** Reseller and implementation channels should wait until onboarding, packaging, support, and partner economics are repeatable.

## 62. Paid acquisition boundaries

**PROHIBITED:** Do not scale paid acquisition before activation, retention, support burden, and conversion measurement are credible.

## 63. Sales pipeline definitions

Use consistent stages: identified, contacted, discovery, qualified, demo, pilot proposed, pilot active, commercial proposal, won, lost, and nurture.

## 64. Revenue model

**PROPOSED:** Recurring SaaS subscription supplemented by justified onboarding, migration, payments, messaging, or premium-service revenue.

## 65. Unit economics framework

Measure gross revenue, infrastructure cost, AI cost, messaging/storage cost, payment costs, onboarding labor, support labor, retention, expansion, and acquisition cost per organization.

## 66. CAC assumptions

**FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED:** No numeric CAC assumption is verified. Establish one only after recording channel-specific sales and marketing cost with attributable wins.

## 67. LTV assumptions

**FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED:** No numeric LTV assumption is verified. Calculate only after credible gross margin, churn, retention, and expansion data exist.

## 68. Gross margin considerations

Track cloud, database, storage, messaging, AI, payment, support, and onboarding costs. High-touch service must not be hidden inside an unrealistic software margin claim.

## 69. Infrastructure-cost considerations

Monitor per-organization database use, file storage, bandwidth, background jobs, logs, AI calls, and third-party integrations.

## 70. Support-cost considerations

Segment support by onboarding, defect, workflow education, migration, data correction, and product gap to understand the true cost to serve.

## 71. Financial planning assumptions

Use conservative scenarios, separate committed cost from optional growth spend, and label all revenue projections as assumptions until contracts or historical evidence exist.

## 72. Bootstrapping strategy

**PROPOSED:** Favor disciplined product proof, founder-led sales, controlled infrastructure, and revenue-funded growth before adding fixed overhead.

## 73. Funding strategy

**FOUNDER DECISION REQUIRED:** External funding is optional, not assumed. Evaluate only when capital meaningfully accelerates a validated growth engine or product moat.

## 74. Investor-readiness boundaries

Do not present speculative traction as fact. Investor materials must reconcile product status, customers, revenue, retention, pipeline, costs, risks, and legal readiness.

## 75. Legal and compliance considerations

Before broad launch, establish appropriate entity, contracts, intellectual-property ownership, tax posture, insurance, payment obligations, and industry-specific compliance review.

## 76. Terms, privacy, and data handling

Publish clear terms, privacy disclosures, subprocessors, retention expectations, deletion procedures, breach response, and customer data ownership.

## 77. Customer trust strategy

Trust comes from truthful claims, stable workflows, visible control, responsive support, secure tenancy, reliable documents, and transparent incident handling.

## 78. Security as a business advantage

Use organization isolation, auditability, review-first changes, secure authentication, and documented controls as practical differentiators—not empty marketing language.

## 79. AI trust and disclosure

Explain where AI is used, what data it considers, what remains review-first, how suggestions are accepted, and which decisions it is prohibited from making automatically.

## 80. Product analytics

Collect only metrics with a defined product or business purpose. Protect tenant privacy and avoid unnecessary surveillance.

## 81. Business metrics

Track activated organizations, time to value, weekly active key roles, completed workflows, support burden, retention, expansion, revenue, gross margin, and pipeline conversion.

## 82. North-star metric

**PROPOSED:** Weekly organizations completing at least one meaningful request-to-revenue workflow or verified operational milestone.

## 83. Activation metrics

Measure successful onboarding, first customer/project, first estimate, first scheduled job, first invoice, and first repeated weekly workflow.

## 84. Retention metrics

Measure organization and role retention, repeated core workflow use, document activity, scheduling activity, and return after onboarding.

## 85. Revenue metrics

Track MRR or ARR when applicable, new revenue, expansion, contraction, churn, collections, discounts, payment fees, and revenue concentration.

## 86. Operational metrics

Track onboarding duration, migration defects, support volume, severity, response time, resolution time, uptime, deployment failures, and incident frequency.

## 87. Beta exit criteria

Beta ends only when target workflows work with real data, critical security and tenancy checks pass, support is manageable, onboarding is repeatable, and pilot customers choose continued use.

## 88. Launch-readiness criteria

Require production deployment, backups, monitoring, support ownership, terms/privacy, pricing, onboarding, migration policy, billing, security evidence, and documented rollback.

## 89. Post-launch review cadence

Review weekly during initial launch, monthly after stabilization, and quarterly for pricing, positioning, retention, roadmap, and competitive changes.

## 90. Business risks

Major risks include overbuilding before validation, weak onboarding, unclear positioning, insufficient support capacity, technical instability, and founder attention fragmentation.

## 91. Competitive risks

Competitors may lower price, simplify onboarding, add AI, or target the same segment. TradeOS must respond through customer evidence and execution, not panic copying.

## 92. Pricing risks

Risks include underpricing support, confusing tiers, excessive add-ons, pricing before value proof, and commitments that cannot support infrastructure or service costs.

## 93. Adoption risks

Contractors may resist migration, data cleanup, workflow change, or staff training. Reduce risk through guided implementation and fast first value.

## 94. Support risks

High-touch founder support may not scale. Repeated issues must become product fixes, documentation, onboarding improvements, or scoped service offerings.

## 95. Infrastructure risks

Unbounded AI, storage, messaging, logging, or integration usage can damage margins and reliability. Measure before offering unlimited plans.

## 96. Founder-decision register

Open decisions include finalized pricing, packaging, beta incentives, trial strategy, funding posture, geographic sequence, trade sequence, support commitments, and launch date.

## 97. Business anti-patterns

Avoid fake traction, unsupported competitor claims, enterprise-first expansion, custom features for every prospect, hidden service costs, unlimited promises, and roadmap-by-loudest-customer.

## 98. Deferred business decisions

Defer enterprise sales, internationalization, reseller channels, broad paid acquisition, complex usage billing, and acquisition/exit planning until the core business is validated.

## 99. Canonical business principles

1. Proof before scale.
2. Contractor outcomes before feature volume.
3. Transparent claims and pricing.
4. Fast first value.
5. Review-first AI.
6. Sustainable support and infrastructure.
7. Real workflow completion over vanity metrics.
8. The core 5–30 technician segment comes first.

## 100. One-page executive summary

TradeOS is being built for growing trade contractors that have outgrown lightweight tools but cannot justify enterprise software complexity and cost. Its commercial advantage should come from connected request-to-revenue workflows, strong estimating and cost intelligence, practical scheduling and field coordination, trustworthy customer documents, owner visibility, and review-first AI.

The initial motion is founder-led and proof-driven. Known contractors and carefully selected pilots should validate onboarding, real workflow completion, retention, support burden, and willingness to pay before broad launch or paid acquisition. Pricing, packaging, trial structure, beta incentives, and funding remain founder decisions and must not be presented as finalized.

TradeOS should grow through regional credibility, contractor referrals, useful content, migration confidence, and strong product evidence. It must avoid becoming a generic ERP, an enterprise-services consultancy, or an AI system that acts without contractor control. The business is ready to scale only when product reliability, tenancy, security, onboarding, support, pricing, legal readiness, unit economics, and customer retention are demonstrated together.
